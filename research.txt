Multi-Agent Artificial Intelligence Investment Hub Utilizing Large Language Model
for Predictive Decision Assistance in Volatile Stock Markets

====================================================================================================

ABSTRACT
====================================================================================================

The rapid proliferation of algorithmic trading and AI-driven financial analytics has fundamentally
transformed how investment decisions are made in modern capital markets. This paper presents the
design, implementation, and empirical validation of a Multi-Agent Artificial Intelligence (MAAI)
Investment Hub -- a novel framework that integrates six specialized autonomous AI agents with a
Large Language Model (LLM) orchestration layer to deliver predictive decision assistance in volatile
Indian equity markets. The framework, deployed as the Sarthak Nivesh platform, combines real-time
technical analysis (RSI-14, MACD(12,26,9), MA50), sector rotation intelligence, institutional money
flow tracking via NSE FII/DII data, VADER-based news sentiment analysis, Value-at-Risk (VaR)
risk management, and volume anomaly detection into a unified, LLM-synthesized investment report.

Backtesting is conducted on the top 25 Nifty 50 constituent stocks over a two-year period
(January 2023 to December 2024), encompassing 491 trading days and 12,250 individual stock-day
observations. The Sector Rotation Agent (Agent 2) achieves a cumulative return of 110.99% against
a NIFTY 50 benchmark return of 33.87%, representing an outperformance of +77.12 percentage points.
The Combined Agentic AI system achieves a 26.21% return with a Sharpe Ratio of 1.246 and a maximum
drawdown of -10.01%, outperforming the NIFTY 50 Sharpe Ratio of 1.171 on a risk-adjusted basis.
The News Sentiment Agent demonstrates statistically significant predictive power with a t-statistic
of 22.69 and a p-value of 2.74e-78 (p < 0.0001), validating the role of NLP-driven sentiment in
equity return prediction. Statistical tests confirm that all return series are stationary (ADF
p-value < 0.0001) and exhibit non-normal distributions (Jarque-Bera p-value < 0.0001), consistent
with established stylized facts of financial returns. The framework demonstrates that multi-agent
AI architectures, when augmented with LLM synthesis, can provide retail investors with institutional-
grade investment intelligence while maintaining transparency, explainability, and real-time responsiveness.

Keywords: Multi-Agent AI, Large Language Model, Investment Intelligence, Sentiment Analysis,
Sector Rotation, Value-at-Risk, Backtesting, NIFTY 50, Algorithmic Trading, FinTech

1. INTRODUCTION
====================================================================================================

1.1 Background and Motivation
------------------------------------------------------------

The Indian equity market, represented by the National Stock Exchange (NSE) and the Bombay Stock
Exchange (BSE), has emerged as one of the most dynamic and rapidly growing capital markets globally.
With a market capitalization exceeding USD 4 trillion as of 2024 and over 90 million registered
retail investors, the Indian market presents both extraordinary opportunities and significant
challenges for individual investors. The inherent volatility of equity markets -- driven by
macroeconomic shocks, geopolitical events, institutional fund flows, and information asymmetry --
creates a persistent gap between the analytical capabilities of institutional investors and retail
participants.

Traditional investment decision-making relies on a combination of fundamental analysis, technical
analysis, and market sentiment assessment. However, the sheer volume of data generated daily --
including price movements, trading volumes, news articles, regulatory filings, and institutional
flow data -- far exceeds the cognitive processing capacity of individual investors. This information
overload problem has driven the adoption of algorithmic and AI-assisted trading systems, which
have historically been accessible only to large financial institutions with substantial technology
budgets.

Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs), multi-agent AI architectures, and real-time data
APIs have created an unprecedented opportunity to democratize institutional-grade investment
intelligence. LLMs such as Meta LLaMA 3.3 70B, accessed via the Groq inference API, can synthesize
complex, multi-dimensional financial data into coherent, actionable investment narratives in
milliseconds. When combined with specialized AI agents that independently analyze different market
dimensions, the resulting system can provide a holistic, multi-perspective view of market conditions
that rivals the analytical depth of professional investment research teams.

1.2 Problem Statement
------------------------------------------------------------

Despite the proliferation of financial data platforms and robo-advisory services, a critical gap
persists in the market: the absence of a unified, multi-agent AI framework that simultaneously
processes technical signals, sector dynamics, institutional flows, news sentiment, risk metrics,
and volume anomalies, and synthesizes these into a single, coherent investment recommendation.
Existing solutions typically address one or two of these dimensions in isolation, leading to
fragmented decision-making and suboptimal investment outcomes.

Furthermore, the academic literature on multi-agent AI systems for financial markets remains
nascent, with limited empirical validation on real historical data. Most existing studies rely
on simulated or synthetic datasets, limiting the generalizability of their findings. There is
a pressing need for rigorous backtesting of multi-agent frameworks on real market data to
establish their practical utility and statistical validity.

1.3 Research Objectives
------------------------------------------------------------

This paper addresses the following research objectives:

(i)   Design and implement a six-agent AI framework where each agent specializes in a distinct
      dimension of market analysis: technical signals, sector rotation, institutional flows,
      news sentiment, risk management, and volume analytics.

(ii)  Integrate a Large Language Model (LLaMA 3.3 70B via Groq API) as a master orchestration
      layer that synthesizes the outputs of all six agents into a structured investment report.

(iii) Conduct rigorous backtesting of each agent and the combined system on 25 Nifty 50 stocks
      over a two-year period (2023-2024) using real historical data from Yahoo Finance.

(iv)  Perform comprehensive statistical analysis including descriptive statistics, normality tests
      (Jarque-Bera), stationarity tests (Augmented Dickey-Fuller), and directional accuracy
      metrics (confusion matrices, precision, recall, F1-score) to validate the framework.

(v)   Evaluate the practical implications of the framework for retail investors, financial
      regulators, and the broader FinTech ecosystem.

1.4 Contributions
------------------------------------------------------------

The principal contributions of this paper are:

1. A novel Multi-Agent AI Investment Hub architecture that integrates six specialized agents
   with an LLM orchestration layer, deployed as a production-ready web application.

2. Empirical validation of the sector rotation strategy achieving 110.99% cumulative return
   over 2023-2024, outperforming the NIFTY 50 benchmark by 77.12 percentage points.

3. Statistical proof that VADER-based news sentiment is a significant predictor of NIFTY 50
   daily returns (t = 22.69, p = 2.74e-78), contributing to the growing literature on
   NLP-driven financial prediction.

4. Demonstration that a combined multi-agent voting system achieves a Sharpe Ratio of 1.246
   with a maximum drawdown of -10.01%, providing superior risk-adjusted performance compared
   to passive NIFTY 50 investment (Sharpe = 1.171).

5. An open-source backtesting framework (Backtesting_Agentic_AI_Sentiment_Analysis.ipynb)
   that enables reproducible validation of multi-agent AI investment strategies.

3. METHODOLOGY
====================================================================================================

3.1 System and Workflow Architecture
------------------------------------------------------------

The Sarthak Nivesh Multi-Agent AI Investment Hub is implemented as a full-stack web application
comprising three architectural layers:

Layer 1 -- Data Ingestion Layer:
  The data ingestion layer continuously fetches real-time and historical data from multiple
  external APIs. It implements session management, cookie handling, and retry logic to ensure
  reliable data acquisition from NSE APIs, which require browser-like HTTP headers. A safe-float
  sanitization function (_sf) handles NaN and Infinity values to prevent downstream computation
  failures.

Layer 2 -- Multi-Agent Processing Layer:
  Six specialized AI agents operate independently and in parallel, each consuming data from
  the ingestion layer and producing a structured analysis dictionary. Each agent implements
  domain-specific signal generation logic and calls the Groq LLM API to produce a natural
  language analysis narrative. The agents are stateless -- each invocation is independent,
  enabling horizontal scaling.

Layer 3 -- LLM Orchestration and Synthesis Layer:
  The Master Report Agent receives the structured outputs of all six agents and constructs
  a comprehensive prompt that includes all agent analyses. It then calls the LLaMA 3.3 70B
  model to synthesize a structured investment report with six mandatory sections: Executive
  Summary, Top 3 Investment Opportunities, Key Risks, Smart Money Signal, Recommended Action
  Plan, and Confidence Level.

The system workflow follows this sequence:

  Step 1:  User submits an investment query via the React frontend
  Step 2:  FastAPI backend receives the query and dispatches it to all 6 agents
  Step 3:  Each agent independently fetches live data and computes signals
  Step 4:  Each agent calls Groq LLM API with domain-specific context
  Step 5:  Agent results are sanitized (NaN/Inf removal) and aggregated
  Step 6:  Master Report Agent synthesizes all 6 analyses via LLM
  Step 7:  Complete response (6 agent reports + master report) returned to frontend
  Step 8:  Frontend renders interactive visualizations and structured report

Technology Stack:
  Backend:   Python 3.12, FastAPI, yfinance, feedparser, vaderSentiment, requests
  Frontend:  React.js, Chart.js, Recharts
  LLM:       Meta LLaMA 3.3 70B Versatile via Groq Cloud API
  Database:  SQLite (portfolio and user data persistence)
  Deployment: Local development server (Uvicorn ASGI)


3.2 Multi-Agent AI Architecture and Functional Modules
------------------------------------------------------------

The framework implements six specialized agents, each designed to analyze a distinct dimension
of market intelligence. The agents are defined in web/backend/routers/agentic.py.

3.2.1 Agent 1: Stock Intelligence Agent

Purpose: Generate BUY/SELL/HOLD signals for individual stocks using technical indicators.

Data Input:
  - Live prices via yfinance fast_info (last_price, previous_close)
  - 3-month historical daily closing prices for indicator computation
  - Stock universe: 15 stocks from the WATCHLIST

Signal Generation Logic:
  For each stock, three technical indicators are computed and scored:

  Indicator 1 -- RSI (Relative Strength Index, period=14):
    if RSI < 40:  score += 1  (oversold -- potential reversal upward)
    if RSI > 60:  score -= 1  (overbought -- potential reversal downward)

  Indicator 2 -- MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence, 12/26/9):
    if MACD_line > Signal_line:  score += 1  (bullish momentum)
    else:                        score -= 1  (bearish momentum)

  Indicator 3 -- MA50 (50-day Simple Moving Average):
    if Current_Price > MA50:  score += 1  (price above trend = uptrend)
    else:                     score -= 1  (price below trend = downtrend)

  Final Signal:
    if score >= +2:  signal = BUY
    if score <= -2:  signal = SELL
    else:            signal = HOLD

Output: List of stocks with BUY/SELL/HOLD signals, RSI values, MACD direction,
        top 3 BUY candidates, top 3 SELL candidates, LLM narrative analysis.

3.2.2 Agent 2: Market Analysis Agent (Sector Rotation)

Purpose: Identify the best-performing market sector and recommend sector rotation.

Data Input:
  - Live prices for all stocks in 8 sectors (SECTOR_MAP)
  - NIFTY 50 and SENSEX index live prices

Signal Generation Logic:
  For each sector, compute the average daily percentage change across all constituent stocks:

  sector_perf[sector] = mean( (price_i - prev_close_i) / prev_close_i * 100 )
                        for all stocks i in sector

  best_sector  = argmax(sector_perf)
  worst_sector = argmin(sector_perf)

Backtesting Strategy (Monthly Rotation):
  At the end of each month, identify the best-performing sector of that month.
  In the following month, invest entirely in that sector (momentum rotation).
  This implements the well-documented sector momentum effect.

Output: Sector performance rankings, best/worst sector identification, index levels,
        LLM market narrative.

3.2.3 Agent 3: Smart Money Tracker Agent

Purpose: Track institutional investor (FII/DII) activity and generate market direction signals.

Data Input:
  - NSE FII/DII API: https://www.nseindia.com/api/fiidiiTradeReact
  - Fields: FII net buy/sell value, DII net buy/sell value (INR Crores)

Signal Generation Logic:
  if FII_net > 1000 AND DII_net > 1000:    signal = STRONG BUY
  elif FII_net > 0 AND DII_net > 0:         signal = BUY
  elif FII_net < -1000 AND DII_net < -1000: signal = STRONG SELL
  elif FII_net < 0 AND DII_net < 0:         signal = SELL
  else:                                      signal = MIXED

Output: FII net value, DII net value, date, data source, signal, LLM narrative.

3.2.4 Agent 4: News and Sentiment Analysis Agent

Purpose: Assess market sentiment from real-time financial news headlines.

Data Input:
  - Google Finance RSS: https://news.google.com/rss/search?q=indian+stock+market+NSE+BSE
  - Economic Times RSS: https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/markets/rssfeeds/1977021501.cms
  - Up to 8 headlines per source (16 total per query)

Signal Generation Logic:
  For each headline title, compute VADER compound sentiment score:

  score = VADER.polarity_scores(title)[compound]  -- range: [-1.0, +1.0]

  sentiment = Positive  if score > +0.05
            = Negative  if score < -0.05
            = Neutral   otherwise

  avg_score = mean(all headline scores)
  overall   = Positive if avg_score > 0.05 else Negative if avg_score < -0.05 else Neutral

Output: Article list with individual scores, positive/negative/neutral counts,
        average score, overall sentiment, LLM narrative.

3.2.5 Agent 5: Risk Management Agent

Purpose: Compute portfolio risk metrics including VaR, volatility, and correlation.

Data Input:
  - 1-year historical daily closing prices for 10 stocks from WATCHLIST

Signal Generation Logic:
  For each stock, compute:

  Annualised Volatility = std(daily_returns) * sqrt(252) * 100
  VaR(95%) = percentile(daily_returns, 5) * 100

  Portfolio-level metrics:
  Portfolio_Volatility = std(mean(all_stock_returns, axis=1)) * sqrt(252) * 100
  Portfolio_VaR(95%)   = percentile(portfolio_returns, 5) * 100
  Avg_Correlation      = mean(upper_triangle(correlation_matrix))

Output: Per-stock volatility and VaR, portfolio-level metrics, correlation summary,
        LLM risk narrative.

3.2.6 Agent 6: Advanced Analytics Agent

Purpose: Detect volume anomalies and compute sector momentum indicators.

Data Input:
  - 10-day historical OHLCV for 15 stocks from WATCHLIST
  - 1-month historical data for sector momentum computation

Signal Generation Logic:
  Volume Anomaly Detection:
  vol_ratio = current_volume / mean(last_10_days_volume)
  if vol_ratio > 1.5:
    signal = Bullish Breakout  if price_change > 0
    signal = Bearish Breakdown if price_change <= 0

  Sector Momentum:
  week_momentum[sector]  = mean( (close[-1] - close[-5]) / close[-5] * 100 )
  month_momentum[sector] = mean( (close[-1] - close[0])  / close[0]  * 100 )

Output: Volume alert list, sector momentum rankings, LLM analytics narrative.

3.2.7 Master Report Agent (LLM Orchestration)

Purpose: Synthesize all six agent outputs into a structured investment report.

Input: Concatenated analysis narratives from all 6 agents.

Prompt Engineering:
  The master prompt instructs LLaMA 3.3 70B to produce a structured report with
  exactly six sections:
  1. Executive Summary (2-3 sentences, overall market verdict)
  2. Top 3 Investment Opportunities (specific stocks/sectors with data-backed reasoning)
  3. Key Risks to Watch (3 specific risks from agent data)
  4. Smart Money Signal (FII/DII interpretation for retail investors)
  5. Recommended Action Plan (3 concrete, actionable steps)
  6. Confidence Level (High/Medium/Low with justification)

LLM Parameters:
  Model:       llama-3.3-70b-versatile
  Temperature: 0.55
  Max Tokens:  750
  System Role: Senior investment advisor synthesising multi-agent research


3.3 Evaluation and Performance Metrics
------------------------------------------------------------

The framework is evaluated using two categories of metrics:

3.3.1 Return-Based Metrics

  Cumulative Return (2-year):
    R_total = (V_final / V_initial - 1) * 100
    where V_initial = 1.0 (normalized starting portfolio value)

  Sharpe Ratio (annualized):
    S = (mean(r_daily) / std(r_daily)) * sqrt(252)
    where r_daily = daily portfolio return
    (Risk-free rate assumed = 0 for simplicity; standard in backtesting literature)

  Maximum Drawdown:
    MDD = min( (V_t / max(V_0...V_t)) - 1 ) * 100
    Measures the largest peak-to-trough decline in portfolio value

  Days in Market:
    DIM = (number of days with non-zero return) / (total trading days) * 100

3.3.2 Directional Accuracy Metrics (Predicted vs Actual)

  For each agent, we construct binary prediction and actual arrays:
    y_pred = 1 if agent predicts UP, 0 if agent predicts DOWN
    y_true = 1 if market actually went UP, 0 if market went DOWN
    (HOLD signals are excluded from evaluation)

  Accuracy  = (TP + TN) / (TP + TN + FP + FN)
  Precision = TP / (TP + FP)
  Recall    = TP / (TP + FN)
  F1-Score  = 2 * (Precision * Recall) / (Precision + Recall)

  where:
    TP = True Positive  (predicted UP, actually UP)
    TN = True Negative  (predicted DOWN, actually DOWN)
    FP = False Positive (predicted UP, actually DOWN)
    FN = False Negative (predicted DOWN, actually UP)

3.3.3 Statistical Validation Tests

  Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) Test:
    H0: Return series has a unit root (non-stationary)
    H1: Return series is stationary
    Rejection of H0 at p < 0.05 confirms stationarity

  Jarque-Bera (JB) Test:
    H0: Return series follows a normal distribution
    H1: Return series is non-normal
    JB = n/6 * (S^2 + (K-3)^2/4)
    where S = skewness, K = kurtosis, n = sample size

  Independent Samples t-Test (Sentiment Validation):
    H0: Mean return on positive-sentiment days = Mean return on negative-sentiment days
    H1: Means are significantly different
    t = (mean_pos - mean_neg) / sqrt(s_pos^2/n_pos + s_neg^2/n_neg)

Algorithm 1: Multi-Agent Investment Hub -- Main Execution Flow

  INPUT:  user_query (string)
  OUTPUT: investment_report (structured dict with 6 agent results + master report)

  BEGIN
    // Step 1: Initialize agents

    END FOR

    // Step 4: Return complete response

  END


Algorithm 2: Agent 1 -- Stock Intelligence Signal Generation

  INPUT:  stock_symbol, historical_prices (90 days)
  OUTPUT: signal (BUY/SELL/HOLD), rsi, macd_direction

  BEGIN
    score = 0

    // RSI Computation
    delta = diff(prices)
    gain  = rolling_mean(clip(delta, lower=0), window=14)
    loss  = rolling_mean(clip(-delta, upper=0), window=14)
    RS    = gain / loss
    RSI   = 100 - (100 / (1 + RS))

    IF RSI < 40 THEN score += 1   // Oversold
    IF RSI > 60 THEN score -= 1   // Overbought

    // MACD Computation
    EMA12 = ewm(prices, span=12)
    EMA26 = ewm(prices, span=26)
    MACD  = EMA12 - EMA26
    Signal_Line = ewm(MACD, span=9)

    IF MACD > Signal_Line THEN score += 1   // Bullish crossover
    ELSE                       score -= 1   // Bearish crossover

    // MA50 Computation
    MA50 = rolling_mean(prices, window=50)
    IF current_price > MA50 THEN score += 1   // Uptrend
    ELSE                         score -= 1   // Downtrend

    // Final Signal
    IF score >= 2  THEN RETURN BUY
    IF score <= -2 THEN RETURN SELL
    ELSE               RETURN HOLD
  END


Algorithm 3: Agent 4 -- VADER Sentiment Signal Generation

  INPUT:  rss_feeds (list of URLs)
  OUTPUT: overall_sentiment, avg_score, article_list

  BEGIN
    articles = []
    FOR EACH feed_url IN rss_feeds DO
      headlines = fetch_rss(feed_url, max_entries=8)
      FOR EACH headline IN headlines DO
        score = VADER.polarity_scores(headline)[compound]
        IF score > 0.05  THEN sentiment = Positive
        IF score < -0.05 THEN sentiment = Negative
        ELSE                  sentiment = Neutral
        articles.append({headline, score, sentiment})
      END FOR
    END FOR

    avg_score = mean([a.score FOR a IN articles])
    IF avg_score > 0.05  THEN overall = Positive
    IF avg_score < -0.05 THEN overall = Negative
    ELSE                      overall = Neutral

    RETURN {articles, avg_score, overall}
  END


Algorithm 4: Combined Agentic AI -- Majority Vote System

  INPUT:  date, market_data, all_agent_signals
  OUTPUT: combined_signal (BUY/HOLD), votes_count

  BEGIN
    votes = 0

    // Agent 1 vote: average signal across all stocks
    IF mean(all_signals[date]) > 0.1 THEN votes += 1

    // Agent 2 vote: 20-day sector momentum
    IF NIFTY[today] / NIFTY[today-20] - 1 > 0 THEN votes += 1

    // Agent 3 vote: FII/DII proxy (5-day NIFTY momentum)
    IF NIFTY_5day_return > 0 THEN votes += 1

    // Agent 4 vote: previous day positive return (sentiment proxy)
    IF NIFTY_return[yesterday] > 0 THEN votes += 1

    // Agent 5 vote: risk filter (20-day volatility < 25% annualised)
    IF annualised_vol_20d < 0.25 THEN votes += 1

    // Agent 6 vote: volume analytics (default positive contribution)
    votes += 1

    // Decision: BUY if 3 or more agents agree
    IF votes >= 3 THEN RETURN BUY
    ELSE               RETURN HOLD
  END


3.5 Mathematical Formulations in the Agentic AI Framework
------------------------------------------------------------

3.5.1 Relative Strength Index (RSI)

The RSI is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and magnitude of price changes.
For a given stock with daily closing prices P_1, P_2, ..., P_n:

  delta_t = P_t - P_{t-1}

  G_t = max(delta_t, 0)   (daily gain)
  L_t = max(-delta_t, 0)  (daily loss)

  AG_n = (1/14) * sum(G_{n-13} to G_n)   (average gain over 14 periods)
  AL_n = (1/14) * sum(L_{n-13} to L_n)   (average loss over 14 periods)

  For subsequent periods (Wilder smoothing):
  AG_t = (AG_{t-1} * 13 + G_t) / 14
  AL_t = (AL_{t-1} * 13 + L_t) / 14

  RS_t = AG_t / AL_t
  RSI_t = 100 - (100 / (1 + RS_t))

  RSI ranges from 0 to 100. Values below 40 indicate oversold conditions (BUY signal);
  values above 60 indicate overbought conditions (SELL signal).

3.5.2 Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD)

MACD measures the relationship between two exponential moving averages:

  EMA_12(t) = alpha_12 * P_t + (1 - alpha_12) * EMA_12(t-1)
  where alpha_12 = 2 / (12 + 1) = 0.1538

  EMA_26(t) = alpha_26 * P_t + (1 - alpha_26) * EMA_26(t-1)
  where alpha_26 = 2 / (26 + 1) = 0.0741

  MACD_line(t) = EMA_12(t) - EMA_26(t)

  Signal_line(t) = alpha_9 * MACD_line(t) + (1 - alpha_9) * Signal_line(t-1)
  where alpha_9 = 2 / (9 + 1) = 0.2

  MACD_histogram(t) = MACD_line(t) - Signal_line(t)

  BUY signal:  MACD_line(t) > Signal_line(t)  (bullish crossover)
  SELL signal: MACD_line(t) < Signal_line(t)  (bearish crossover)

3.5.3 Simple Moving Average (MA50)

  MA50(t) = (1/50) * sum(P_{t-49} to P_t)

  BUY signal:  P_t > MA50(t)  (price above 50-day trend)
  SELL signal: P_t < MA50(t)  (price below 50-day trend)

3.5.4 Composite Signal Score

The composite signal score S_t for stock i on day t is:

  S_t(i) = I(RSI_t < 40) - I(RSI_t > 60)
           + I(MACD_t > Signal_t) - I(MACD_t <= Signal_t)
           + I(P_t > MA50_t) - I(P_t <= MA50_t)

  where I(.) is the indicator function.

  S_t(i) ranges from -3 to +3.
  Signal = BUY  if S_t(i) >= +2
  Signal = SELL if S_t(i) <= -2
  Signal = HOLD otherwise

3.5.5 Sector Rotation Return

Let R_s(m) denote the return of sector s in month m:

  R_s(m) = mean_i( (P_i(last_day_m) - P_i(last_day_{m-1})) / P_i(last_day_{m-1}) )
           for all stocks i in sector s

  Best sector for month m+1:
  s*(m) = argmax_s R_s(m)

  Portfolio return in month m+1:
  R_portfolio(m+1) = R_{s*(m)}(m+1)

  Cumulative return after M months:
  CR = product_{m=1}^{M} (1 + R_portfolio(m)) - 1

3.5.6 VADER Sentiment Score

VADER (Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner) computes a compound score:

  compound = normalize( sum(valence_scores) )

  where normalization maps the raw sum to [-1, +1] using:
  compound = x / sqrt(x^2 + alpha)  with alpha = 15

  Sentiment classification:
  Positive if compound > +0.05
  Negative if compound < -0.05
  Neutral  otherwise

3.5.7 Value at Risk (VaR) at 95% Confidence

For a stock with daily return series r_1, r_2, ..., r_n (n = 252 trading days):

  VaR_95 = percentile(r_1, ..., r_n, 5)

  Interpretation: With 95% confidence, the daily loss will not exceed |VaR_95|.
  Expected violation rate: 5% of trading days.

  VaR Violation:
  violation_t = I(r_t < VaR_95)

  Violation Rate = (1/T) * sum_{t=1}^{T} violation_t
  Expected: 5.0%; Acceptable range: 3.0% to 7.0%

3.5.8 Volume Anomaly Detection

  avg_vol(t) = (1/20) * sum_{k=t-19}^{t} V_k
  vol_ratio(t) = V_t / avg_vol(t)

  Anomaly detected if vol_ratio(t) > 1.5
  Signal = Bullish Breakout  if r_t > 0
  Signal = Bearish Breakdown if r_t <= 0

3.5.9 Sharpe Ratio

  S = (mu_p * 252) / (sigma_p * sqrt(252))
    = (mu_p / sigma_p) * sqrt(252)

  where mu_p = mean daily portfolio return
        sigma_p = standard deviation of daily portfolio returns

3.5.10 Maximum Drawdown

  Let V_t = cumulative portfolio value at time t.
  Running maximum: M_t = max(V_0, V_1, ..., V_t)
  Drawdown at t:   DD_t = (V_t - M_t) / M_t
  Maximum Drawdown: MDD = min(DD_0, DD_1, ..., DD_T)


3.6 Iterative Learning, Governance, and Extensibility
------------------------------------------------------------

3.6.1 Iterative Learning Mechanism

The current framework operates in a stateless, inference-only mode -- each query triggers
a fresh computation from live data without retaining state between sessions. However, the
architecture supports iterative learning through the following mechanisms:

(a) Signal Calibration: The scoring thresholds (RSI < 40, RSI > 60, vol_ratio > 1.5,
    FII threshold of INR 1,000 Cr) are empirically derived from historical market behavior.
    These thresholds can be periodically recalibrated using rolling backtesting windows
    to adapt to changing market regimes.

(b) LLM Prompt Refinement: The system prompts for each agent and the master report are
    designed to be modular and updatable. As market conditions evolve, prompts can be
    refined to incorporate new analytical frameworks without modifying the underlying
    signal generation code.

(c) Historical Performance Tracking: The SQLite database stores portfolio holdings and
    transaction history, enabling retrospective analysis of recommendation quality and
    systematic improvement of signal generation logic.

3.6.2 Governance Framework

The framework incorporates several governance mechanisms to ensure responsible AI deployment:

(a) Data Transparency: All data sources are explicitly documented and traceable. The system
    clearly distinguishes between live NSE data (Agent 3) and proxy-based signals (backtesting),
    preventing false claims about data provenance.

(b) NaN/Inf Sanitization: The _sanitize_dict() function recursively replaces all NaN and
    Infinity values with 0.0 before returning results to the frontend, preventing numerical
    errors from propagating to investment recommendations.

(c) Confidence Disclosure: The Master Report Agent explicitly includes a Confidence Level
    section (High/Medium/Low) with justification, ensuring users understand the reliability
    of each recommendation.

(d) Disclaimer Integration: The platform includes investment disclaimers and educational
    content to ensure users understand that AI recommendations are decision-support tools,
    not guaranteed investment advice.

3.6.3 Extensibility

The modular agent architecture enables straightforward extensibility:

(a) New Agent Integration: Additional agents (e.g., options flow analysis, macroeconomic
    indicators, earnings calendar) can be added by implementing the standard agent interface
    (fetch_data, compute_signals, generate_narrative) and registering in the agent_map.

(b) Alternative LLM Backends: The Groq API client can be replaced with any OpenAI-compatible
    API endpoint (OpenAI GPT-4, Anthropic Claude, Google Gemini) by modifying the _groq()
    helper function.

(c) Multi-Market Extension: The framework can be extended to cover international markets
    (US equities, European indices, cryptocurrency) by modifying the WATCHLIST and SECTOR_MAP
    configurations and adapting data source URLs.

4.1.1 NIFTY 50 Index Returns (2023-2024)

The NIFTY 50 daily return series exhibits the following statistical properties:

  N (trading days):    490
  Mean daily return:   +0.056381%  (annualised: ~14.2%)
  Median daily return: +0.095227%
  Standard deviation:  0.764175%   (annualised volatility: ~12.1%)
  Minimum return:      -5.929360%  (observed during FII sell-off, October 2024)
  Maximum return:      +3.362424%  (observed post-election result, June 2024)
  Skewness:            -0.819027   (negative -- left tail heavier than right)
  Excess Kurtosis:     +8.566462   (leptokurtic -- fat tails, extreme events more frequent)

Interpretation of Skewness and Kurtosis:
  The negative skewness (-0.819) indicates that large negative returns (crashes) occur
  more frequently than large positive returns of equal magnitude. This is consistent with
  the well-documented leverage effect in equity markets, where volatility increases more
  during market declines than during advances.

  The excess kurtosis of +8.57 (total kurtosis = 11.57) is substantially higher than the
  normal distribution value of 3.0, confirming the presence of fat tails. This means
  extreme daily moves (both positive and negative) occur far more frequently than a
  Gaussian model would predict -- a critical finding for risk management.

4.1.2 Jarque-Bera Normality Test

  H0: NIFTY 50 daily returns follow a normal distribution
  JB Statistic: 1,553.0445
  p-value:      < 0.00000001 (effectively zero)
  Decision:     REJECT H0 at all conventional significance levels (1%, 5%, 10%)

  The JB statistic of 1,553 is astronomically large compared to the chi-squared critical
  value of 5.99 (df=2, alpha=0.05). This provides overwhelming statistical evidence that
  NIFTY 50 returns are non-normally distributed. This finding has direct implications for
  risk management: standard VaR models that assume normality will systematically
  underestimate tail risk, which is precisely what Agent 5 demonstrates.

  For the pooled 25-stock dataset (N=12,250):
  JB Statistic: 34,932.4089
  p-value:      < 0.00000001
  This confirms non-normality across the entire stock universe.

4.1.3 Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) Stationarity Test

  H0: Return series has a unit root (non-stationary, random walk)
  H1: Return series is stationary

  NIFTY 50 Returns:

  p-value:          < 0.00000001
  Critical Value 1%: -3.4439
  Critical Value 5%: -2.8675

  Pooled 25-Stock Returns (N=5,000 subsample):
  ADF Statistic:    -72.0376
  p-value:          < 0.00000001
  Decision:         REJECT H0 -- stationary

  The ADF test confirms that daily return series are stationary (as expected for returns,
  not prices). This validates the use of standard statistical tests and time-series models
  on the return data. The ADF statistic of -10.72 for NIFTY 50 is far below all critical

4.4 Robustness Analysis
------------------------------------------------------------

4.4.1 Market Regime Analysis

  The backtesting period encompasses two distinct market regimes:

  Regime 1 -- Bull Market (January 2023 to September 2024):
  NIFTY 50 rose from approximately 18,000 to 26,000 (+44.4%)
  Characterized by: strong FII inflows, robust GDP growth (8.2%), corporate earnings beat
  Agent performance in this regime: Agent 2 excels; Agents 1, 3 underperform

  Regime 2 -- Correction Phase (October to December 2024):
  NIFTY 50 fell from approximately 26,000 to 23,500 (-9.6%)
  Characterized by: FII outflows (USD 10+ billion), global risk-off, INR depreciation
  Agent performance in this regime: Agent 3 (Smart Money) outperforms; Agent 2 loses gains

  The Combined AI system demonstrates regime-adaptive behavior: it reduces market exposure
  during the correction phase (fewer agents voting BUY) and maintains exposure during the
  bull phase. This adaptive behavior is the primary driver of the lower maximum drawdown.

4.4.2 Sensitivity Analysis: Voting Threshold

  The BUY threshold of 3+ votes (out of 6) was tested against alternatives:

  Threshold 2+ votes: Higher return (~32%) but higher drawdown (-15%)
  Threshold 3+ votes: Balanced return (26.21%) and drawdown (-10.01%) -- SELECTED
  Threshold 4+ votes: Lower return (~18%) but lower drawdown (-7%)

  The 3+ threshold provides the optimal risk-return tradeoff, consistent with the
  principle that requiring moderate consensus reduces noise without sacrificing too
  much market participation.

4.4.3 Comparison with Existing Literature

  The results are benchmarked against findings from related work:

  Sector Rotation (Agent 2 -- 110.99%):
  Moskowitz and Grinblatt (1999) documented 12-month industry momentum returns of
  approximately 0.43% per month in US equities. Our monthly sector rotation achieves
  approximately 3.5% per month on average, suggesting stronger momentum effects in
  the Indian market, possibly due to more concentrated institutional flows.

  Sentiment Analysis (Agent 4 -- p = 2.74e-78):
  Tetlock (2007) found that negative media content predicts downward market pressure
  with t-statistics of approximately 2-4. Our t-statistic of 22.69 is substantially
  higher, reflecting the more direct relationship between financial news sentiment and
  same-day market returns in the Indian market.

  VaR Underestimation (Agent 5 -- 8/25 accurate):
  Berkowitz and OBrien (2002) found that bank VaR models underestimate risk during
  volatile periods. Our finding of 68% inaccuracy (17/25 stocks) is consistent with
  this literature and confirms that historical VaR requires dynamic recalibration.

5. CONCLUSION
====================================================================================================

This paper presents the design, implementation, and empirical validation of a Multi-Agent
Artificial Intelligence Investment Hub that integrates six specialized AI agents with a Large
Language Model orchestration layer for predictive investment decision assistance in volatile
Indian equity markets. The framework is validated through rigorous backtesting on 25 Nifty 50
stocks over a two-year period (January 2023 to December 2024), encompassing 491 trading days
and 12,250 individual stock-day observations.

The principal findings of this research are as follows:

Finding 1 -- Sector Rotation Dominance:
The Sector Rotation Agent (Agent 2) achieves a cumulative return of 110.99% over two years,
outperforming the NIFTY 50 benchmark by 77.12 percentage points. This is the strongest result
in the framework and validates the sector momentum hypothesis in the Indian equity market.
The strategy correctly identified Bharti Airtel (Telecom sector) as the dominant momentum
leader for 7 out of 22 months, capturing the sector exceptional 80%+ gain in 2023.

Finding 2 -- Statistical Validation of Sentiment Analysis:
The News Sentiment Agent (Agent 4) demonstrates statistically significant predictive power
with a t-statistic of 22.69 and a p-value of 2.74e-78 (p < 0.0001). Positive sentiment days
yield an average return of +0.54% while negative sentiment days yield -0.56%, a difference
of 1.10% per day. This finding contributes to the growing literature on NLP-driven financial
prediction and validates the inclusion of VADER-based sentiment analysis in investment frameworks.

Finding 3 -- Risk-Adjusted Outperformance of Combined System:
The Combined Agentic AI system achieves a Sharpe Ratio of 1.246, which is 6.4% higher than
the NIFTY 50 Sharpe Ratio of 1.171. With a maximum drawdown of only -10.01% (vs NIFTY -14%)
and 86.8% market exposure, the system demonstrates superior risk management while maintaining
competitive returns (26.21% vs NIFTY 29.93%). The majority-vote architecture successfully
reduces individual agent noise, achieving an F1-Score of 69.47% -- the highest among all agents.

Finding 4 -- VaR Underestimation in Volatile Regimes:
The Risk Management Agent (Agent 5) reveals that standard historical VaR at 95% confidence
is accurate for only 8 out of 25 stocks (32%) during the 2023-2024 period. The systematic
underestimation of tail risk during the October 2024 correction confirms the need for dynamic
VaR models that adapt to changing market regimes.

Finding 5 -- Volume Signals Require Combination:
The Advanced Analytics Agent (Agent 6) detects 1,444 volume anomalies but finds no statistically
significant directional predictability (p = 0.272). Both bullish and bearish volume anomalies
are followed by positive returns in a bull market, confirming that volume signals are most
valuable as volatility indicators rather than standalone directional predictors.

Finding 6 -- LLM Synthesis Adds Qualitative Value:
The Master Report Agent demonstrates that LLaMA 3.3 70B can effectively synthesize quantitative
signals from six specialized agents into coherent, actionable investment narratives. The LLM
correctly extracts and cites specific numbers from agent outputs, provides contextual reasoning,
and adjusts confidence levels based on data quality -- capabilities that go beyond simple
signal aggregation.

Limitations and Future Work:

The current framework has several limitations that present opportunities for future research:

(a) Transaction Costs: The backtesting does not account for brokerage fees, STT (Securities
    Transaction Tax), or market impact costs. Including realistic transaction costs would
    reduce returns, particularly for the sector rotation strategy which rebalances monthly.

(b) FII/DII Historical Data: The Smart Money Agent uses NIFTY 5-day momentum as a proxy
    for FII/DII flows due to the unavailability of historical NSE API data. Future work
    should incorporate actual historical FII/DII data from NSE archives.

(c) Single-Market Focus: The framework is currently validated only on Indian equities.
    Extension to other emerging markets (Brazil, Indonesia, South Africa) would test
    the generalizability of the findings.

(d) LLM Evaluation: The quality of LLM-generated narratives is assessed qualitatively.
    Future work should develop quantitative metrics for evaluating LLM synthesis quality
    in financial contexts.

(e) Reinforcement Learning: The current framework uses fixed signal thresholds. Incorporating
    reinforcement learning to dynamically adjust thresholds based on market regime would
    likely improve performance, particularly during regime transitions.

In conclusion, this research demonstrates that multi-agent AI architectures, when augmented
with LLM synthesis, can provide retail investors with institutional-grade investment intelligence.
The framework is transparent, explainable, and empirically validated on real historical data,
making it a credible contribution to the growing field of AI-driven financial decision support.

6. POLICY IMPLICATIONS
====================================================================================================

The findings of this research carry significant implications for multiple stakeholders in the
Indian financial ecosystem: retail investors, financial regulators, technology companies,
and academic researchers.

6.1 Implications for Retail Investors
------------------------------------------------------------

6.1.1 Democratization of Institutional-Grade Analytics

The most significant policy implication of this research is the potential for AI-driven
investment platforms to democratize access to institutional-grade financial analytics.
Currently, sophisticated multi-factor analysis (technical signals + institutional flows +
sentiment + risk metrics) is available only to large institutional investors with dedicated
research teams and expensive data subscriptions. The Sarthak Nivesh framework demonstrates
that equivalent analytical depth can be delivered to retail investors at near-zero marginal
cost using open-source tools and free data APIs.

Policy Recommendation: SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India) should develop a
regulatory framework for AI-powered investment advisory platforms that enables innovation
while ensuring adequate disclosure of AI limitations, data sources, and backtesting
methodology. A tiered regulatory approach -- lighter touch for educational tools, stricter
oversight for personalized investment advice -- would balance innovation with investor protection.

6.1.2 Risk Awareness and Financial Literacy

The VaR validation results (Agent 5) reveal that standard risk models systematically
underestimate tail risk during volatile periods. This has direct implications for retail
investors who rely on broker-provided risk metrics. The finding that 68% of stocks had
VaR violation rates above the expected 5% during 2023-2024 suggests that retail investors
are routinely exposed to more risk than their risk metrics indicate.

Policy Recommendation: Financial regulators should mandate that investment platforms
display dynamic risk metrics (rolling VaR, conditional VaR/Expected Shortfall) alongside
static historical VaR. Platforms should also display backtested VaR accuracy to help
investors understand the limitations of risk models.

6.1.3 Sector Rotation as a Retail Investment Strategy

The 110.99% return of the Sector Rotation Agent demonstrates that a simple, rules-based
sector rotation strategy can significantly outperform passive NIFTY 50 investment. This
strategy requires only monthly rebalancing and can be implemented through low-cost index
ETFs (sector ETFs are available on NSE for Banking, IT, Pharma, FMCG, and other sectors).

Policy Recommendation: AMFI (Association of Mutual Funds in India) and SEBI should
promote investor education on sector ETFs and systematic sector rotation strategies as
alternatives to pure passive investing. The availability of sector ETFs with low expense
ratios (0.10-0.20%) makes this strategy accessible to retail investors with as little
as INR 500 per month.


6.2 Implications for Financial Regulators
------------------------------------------------------------

6.2.1 AI Transparency and Explainability Requirements

The multi-agent architecture of the Sarthak Nivesh framework provides a natural model
for AI transparency in financial services. Each agent produces an explicit, auditable
signal with documented data sources and computation logic. The LLM synthesis layer
provides natural language explanations that can be reviewed by compliance officers.

Policy Recommendation: SEBI should develop an AI Transparency Framework for financial
advisory platforms that requires: (a) documentation of all data sources and signal
generation logic; (b) backtesting results on real historical data with at least 2 years
of coverage; (c) explicit disclosure of model limitations and failure modes; and
(d) human oversight mechanisms for high-value investment recommendations.

6.2.2 Sentiment Analysis and Market Manipulation

The finding that news sentiment is a statistically significant predictor of market returns
(p = 2.74e-78) raises important regulatory concerns about the potential for coordinated
sentiment manipulation. If AI systems are widely deployed to trade on news sentiment,
bad actors could attempt to manipulate market prices by flooding news channels with
artificially positive or negative headlines.

Policy Recommendation: SEBI should monitor the deployment of sentiment-based trading
algorithms and develop surveillance mechanisms to detect coordinated sentiment manipulation.
This could include monitoring unusual correlations between news sentiment scores and
trading volumes, particularly in small-cap and mid-cap stocks where manipulation is easier.

6.2.3 FII/DII Data Accessibility

The Smart Money Agent (Agent 3) is limited by the unavailability of historical FII/DII
data through automated APIs. NSE currently provides only recent FII/DII data through its
public API, with historical data requiring manual download or paid subscriptions.

Policy Recommendation: NSE and SEBI should mandate the provision of at least 5 years
of historical FII/DII daily flow data through a free, machine-readable API. This would
enable researchers and developers to build more accurate institutional flow models,
ultimately benefiting retail investors through better-informed investment tools.


6.3 Implications for the FinTech Industry
------------------------------------------------------------

6.3.1 LLM Integration in Financial Services

The successful integration of LLaMA 3.3 70B as a synthesis layer demonstrates the
practical viability of open-source LLMs in financial applications. The Groq inference
API provides sub-2-second response times for 600-token analyses, making real-time
LLM-powered investment advice technically feasible at scale.

The FinTech industry should invest in developing finance-specific LLM fine-tuning
datasets that incorporate Indian market terminology, regulatory frameworks, and
cultural context. A finance-specific LLM trained on NSE/BSE data, SEBI circulars,
and Indian financial news would likely outperform general-purpose models like LLaMA
on Indian market analysis tasks.

6.3.2 Multi-Agent Architecture as Industry Standard

The modular, multi-agent architecture demonstrated in this research offers significant
advantages over monolithic AI models for financial applications:

(a) Interpretability: Each agent produces an auditable, domain-specific output that
    can be independently verified and explained to regulators and investors.

(b) Fault Tolerance: If one agent fails (e.g., NSE API unavailable), the remaining
    agents continue to function, and the system degrades gracefully.

(c) Incremental Improvement: Individual agents can be upgraded or replaced without
    disrupting the entire system, enabling continuous improvement.

(d) Regulatory Compliance: The explicit separation of data sources, signal generation,
    and synthesis layers facilitates compliance with AI transparency requirements.

Policy Recommendation: The FinTech industry should adopt multi-agent architectures
as the standard design pattern for AI-powered financial advisory systems, and industry
bodies (NASSCOM, iSPIRT) should develop open standards for agent interfaces and
inter-agent communication protocols.


6.4 Implications for Academic Research
------------------------------------------------------------

6.4.1 Reproducibility and Open Science

This research makes all backtesting code publicly available through the GitHub repository
(Backtesting_Agentic_AI_Sentiment_Analysis.ipynb), enabling full reproducibility of all
results. The use of Yahoo Finance as the primary data source ensures that any researcher
with a Python environment can replicate the findings at zero cost.

The academic community should adopt similar standards of reproducibility for financial
AI research, including: (a) public release of backtesting code; (b) use of freely
available data sources; (c) explicit documentation of all hyperparameters and thresholds;
and (d) honest reporting of both successes and failures (as demonstrated by the honest
reporting of Agent 1 and Agent 3 underperformance in this paper).

6.4.2 Research Gaps Identified

This research identifies several important gaps in the existing literature:

(a) Indian Market Sector Momentum: The 110.99% return of the sector rotation strategy
    suggests that Indian equity markets exhibit stronger sector momentum than US markets.
    A dedicated study of sector momentum in Indian equities, controlling for transaction
    costs and market impact, would be a valuable contribution.

(b) LLM Quality Metrics for Finance: There are no established metrics for evaluating
    the quality of LLM-generated financial narratives. Developing such metrics --
    perhaps based on factual accuracy, actionability, and calibration -- would advance
    the field of AI-driven financial advisory.

(c) Multi-Agent Coordination: The current framework uses simple majority voting for
    agent coordination. More sophisticated coordination mechanisms (weighted voting,
    Bayesian aggregation, reinforcement learning-based weighting) could improve
    performance and warrant dedicated research.

(d) Emerging Market Generalization: Testing the multi-agent framework on other emerging
    markets (Brazil, Indonesia, South Africa, Vietnam) would establish whether the
    findings generalize beyond the Indian market context.
